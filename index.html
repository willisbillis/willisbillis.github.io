About Me

I'm a bioinformatics analyst currently working between the Boss-Scharer and Kohlmeier labs at Emory University's Department of Microbiology and Immunology, specializing in single-cell bioinformatics, systems biology, and graph theory applications.

My path to bioinformatics began in the cosmos. I earned my Bachelor of Science in Astrophysics from The University of Georgia in 2020, where I spent my undergraduate years studying computational fluid dynamics of interstellar hydrogen clouds that fuel star formation in the Milky Way under Robin Shelton's mentorship. My research journey took me from remote observations at Kitt Peak National Observatory and Cerro Tololo Inter-American Observatory—quantifying dark matter in spiral galaxies during high school with Agnes Scott College's Amy Lovell—to spending a southern hemisphere summer in La Serena, Chile, studying Type Ia Supernovae through an NSF Research Experiences for Undergraduates program.

The analytical and computational skills I developed studying the universe's largest structures now help me explore some of biology's smallest mysteries. Since joining Emory in 2021, I've focused on developing collaborative software tools and machine learning approaches for genomic data analysis, bridging computational methods with biological discoveries.

Outside the lab, I'm a multi-instrumentalist (piano, harmonica, ukulele, and trumpet), passionate traveler who loves connecting with people from different cultures, and creative cook who treats the kitchen as another space for experimentation. I share my home with my partner and two cats with wonderfully layered personalities—Kiwi and Theo—who graciously allow us to live in their domain.

    MAPseq Pipeline

    This repository provides an end-to-end open source pipeline for the processing of MAPseq (Marker Assisted Programming sequencing) data. The repository is primarily written in R, with supporting scripts in Shell and Python to facilitate various stages of data analysis. Its modular structure allows users to efficiently process, analyze, and interpret MAPseq datasets in a reproducible manner.

    Magnetic Percolation Art

    An artistic exploration of percolation theory through computational visualization. This project generates beautiful, magnetic-field-inspired patterns by simulating percolation processes and rendering them as digital art.     Network Percolation

    An interactive browser-based simulation of network percolation theory, exploring how a network transitions from a disconnected to a connected state as edges are added. Built with WebGL for real-time rendering, the simulation lets users adjust bond/site occupation probabilities and visualize the emergence of a spanning cluster — the core phenomenon behind percolation thresholds in physics, epidemiology, and materials science.

    View on GitHub →

    Strange Attractors — Interactive 3D Chaos Explorer

    An interactive 3D visualization gallery for exploring strange attractors and chaotic dynamical systems. Built with WebGL (Three.js), it renders real-time trajectories of seven classic attractors (Lorenz, Rössler, Thomas, Aizawa, Chen, Halvorsen, and Dadras) using 4th-order Runge-Kutta integration. Features include adjustable parameters, multiple color schemes, butterfly-effect mode, gallery auto-cycle, and screenshot export — all running at 60 fps directly in the browser with no installation required.
